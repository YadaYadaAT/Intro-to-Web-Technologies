ABOUT US

Below are 10 commonly asked questions about praying mantises.

Praying mantises are carnivorous insects that primarily feed on other insects. Their diet includes flies, crickets, moths, and other small insects.

The lifespan of a praying mantis can vary depending on the species, but on average, they live for about 6 to 12 months. Some larger species may live longer.

While praying mantises are not aggressive towards humans, they may bite if they feel threatened. However, their bites are not harmful and are more of a defensive mechanism.

Female praying mantises are known for their cannibalistic behavior during mating. After mating, the female may consume the male, though this behavior is not universal among all species.

Yes, most praying mantises are capable of flight. They have wings, but not all species are strong fliers. Some mantises use their wings primarily for short-distance flight.

Yes, praying mantises are considered beneficial in gardens and agricultural settings as they help control populations of harmful insects by preying on them.

The iconic "praying" posture is a hunting stance. The mantis uses this posture to camouflage itself among plants while waiting for prey. It is not a position used for prayer or meditation.

Praying mantises can change color, adapting to their surroundings. This ability helps them blend into their environment, making it easier to ambush unsuspecting prey.

Yes, praying mantises can be kept as pets. They are relatively low-maintenance and fascinating to observe. However, it's important to provide them with a suitable habitat and a varied diet.

Praying mantises, in general, are not considered endangered. However, like many insects, they may face threats from habitat loss, pesticide use, and other environmental changes. Certain species may be more vulnerable than others.
